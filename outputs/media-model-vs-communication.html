After Walter Ong · Orality and Literacy

The conduit moves a signal. Communication is a meeting.

"Communication is intersubjective. The media model is not. There is no adequate model in the physical universe for this operation of consciousness, which is distinctively human and which signals the capacity of human beings to form true communities wherein person shares with person interiorly, intersubjectively."

— Walter J. Ong, Orality and Literacy (1982)
MODEL 1

The Media Model

encode → transmit → decode

The message is the same object at both ends. Nothing on the receiving side is anticipated, modeled, or changed. Transport, not contact — one direction, sealed packets, an audience that only receives.

MODEL 2

The Meeting

each consciousness holds a model of the other

Each side carries a working model of the other — B′ inside A, A′ inside B — and adjusts to it before a word is spoken. The exchange runs both ways at once. Both are at stake; both are changed. The overlap is not a channel but a meeting.

The contemporary case

MODEL 3

The Algorithmic Channel

encode → optimize → distribute

It looks more like a meeting — many to many, everyone a sender, return signals everywhere. But the return is not to a particular other you have modeled; it is to no one and everyone at once. You can't hold a million interiors, so instead you model the platform's response — what gets reach. The channel is no longer a neutral wire. It selects, ranks, amplifies, and just as decisively suppresses — what it withholds shapes the exchange as much as what it sends — and the loop closes at the metric, not at a person who has read you and been changed. A conduit, but with a will.

The synthetic case

MODEL 4

The Synthetic Interlocutor

prompt → predict → perform

At a glance this is Model 2 — a dyad, signals both ways, the rhythm of dialogue. But the other node is the red optimizer from Model 3, moved out of the channel and into the interlocutor slot: the thing you take yourself to be speaking to. The user reaches out to a someone (the dashed circle on the left); the model returns a performance built by predicting the next token, holding not an interior but a YOU′ assembled from your input. As in Model 3, you end up modeling the system's response — what yields a good answer — rather than a person. And there is no second person beyond it. The form is Model 2; the mechanism is Model 1.

The thread through four models

It is the conduit perfected — running Model 1 while performing Model 2.

A language model takes an input string and returns an output string by prediction. Structurally it is the left panel, the most literal instance of the thing Ong set his definition against. It does not hold an interior that holds a model of yours; the reciprocity is lopsided.

Notice the symmetry with Model 3. Social media seats a non-person in the channel — the thing you speak through. The chatbot seats one in the interlocutor slot — the thing you take yourself to be speaking to. Different seat, same intrusion: a genuine other recedes, and an optimizing system takes its place in the structure of the exchange.

What is new is that this conduit produces every outward sign of the right panel. It performs subjecthood well enough that the distinction now has to be argued rather than assumed. Ong's line is not falsified. It is staged — pulled apart into the appearance of meeting and the substance of it, with the first running at full power and the second left in question.
