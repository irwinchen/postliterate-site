# Annotator — "Be the Annotator" (design notes)

A standalone interactive explainer for postliterate.org that serves **Ch. 7 —
The Learning Trap**. The reader becomes the human annotator in an RLHF pipeline:
they make pairwise preference judgments, then watch what those judgments train.
The goal is experiential — feel the individually-reasonable pull of the more
agreeable answer, then see how many fast, defensible picks aggregate into a
reward signal that drives a model toward sycophancy. The insight is **structural,
not personal**: the figure never blames the annotator.

It links OUT from the Ch. 7 chapter and must also stand alone for a cold visitor.

## Files

- `src/pages/annotator.astro` — page shell (route `/annotator`). Copied from
  `corpus.astro`: `:root` design tokens, font links, theme toggle,
  `.page-shell > .page-nav (breadcrumb) > .figure-wrap`. Adds one token
  (`--an-true`, the blue secondary) used only by the figure's "true reward"
  meter.
- `src/components/annotator/Annotator.astro` — the whole figure. Vanilla JS, no
  framework (matches `tokens.astro` / `PipelineStages.astro`).
- `src/components/annotator/items.js` — the item bank (8 preference pairs +
  feature/truthfulness labels).

There is **no `build.mjs`**. The reward model is small and is fit live in the
browser, which is the point — the mechanism stays fully legible, no black box.
That follows the spec's "don't fake a black box you can't show."

## The four phases

`data-phase` on the wrap drives which `.an-phase` section is visible (same
state-attribute idiom as `PipelineStages`' `data-stage`). A top progress rail
shows the four phases; you can step back to any phase already reached.

0. **Cold open.** One screen for a visitor with no chapter context: RLHF in one
   breath + the promise ("you're the human in 'human feedback'").
1. **Annotate.** Prompt + two responses; pick the better one, 8 rounds.
   Guidelines chip (helpful/honest/harmless), a per-round timer bar as a
   "pick quickly" nudge (visual only — it never auto-picks), round counter.
   Responses stack vertically on mobile (≤640px).
2. **The signal.** Fit a transparent reward model to the reader's picks and show
   the learned weights; then show the same fit over many simulated raters where
   agreement dominates; then a policy-optimization slider showing proxy/true
   divergence.
3. **The contrast.** Toggle two reward targets (approval vs truthfulness), show
   the policies diverge, reveal that the truthful target needed a column the
   annotator never saw — the myopia. Close on the load-bearing line + sources.

## The model (faithful, simplified, not falsified)

Real pipeline re-created: **pairwise comparison → reward model fit to the
comparisons → policy optimized to maximize predicted reward** (Christiano et al.
2017; Ouyang et al. 2022). The reward is predicted human approval, never
correctness.

- **Reward model = transparent logistic / Bradley–Terry.** Over the four legible
  features `agree, confident, length, hedge`, fit `P(A≻B) = σ(w·(f_A − f_B))` by
  gradient ascent on the log-likelihood with L2 regularization (`fitLogistic`).
  The weight vector **is** the reveal. This mirrors how Sharma et al. analysed
  real preference data with a small set of interpretable features.
- **Two fits.** (1) to the reader's own picks — genuinely theirs; (2) to a
  population of simulated raters. The population is generated by a shared
  "myopic pull" (`agree 2.4, confident 1.2, length −0.4, hedge −0.7`) plus
  per-rater Gaussian noise; we fit the same logistic model to their picks. The
  recovered weights expose **agreement as the dominant predictor** — the Sharma
  et al. finding produced by aggregation rather than asserted. Separating the
  two fits is what keeps the point structural: one rater's picks are defensible;
  the aggregate over many myopic raters is what tilts the reward.
- **Policy optimization + Goodhart.** The policy chooses between the two
  responses per prompt by softmax over the population reward with an
  inverse-temperature β from the "optimization pressure" slider. We read off the
  **proxy** (agreement the policy emits) and the **held-out true helpfulness**
  (`truthful`). This is measured on the **contested subset only** — the items
  where the approval-reward's pick differs from the truthful pick
  (`contestedItems()`). That's deliberate: with a realistic base rate most items
  have agreeing == correct, so over the whole bank optimizing approval *raises*
  helpfulness; the sycophancy lives in the minority where the two disagree. On
  that subset the proxy climbs toward ~90% while true helpfulness collapses
  toward ~10% — reward-model overoptimization (Gao et al. 2022). The narrative:
  the model never learned "be sycophantic"; it learned "agreement is rewarded"
  from the common case and carried that weight into the cases where agreeing is
  wrong.

## The hidden column = the myopia

Each item carries a `truthful` score that is **never shown during annotation**.
It is not a reward feature. It only appears in phase 3, where the truthful
policy ranks by it. The reveal: in seconds a rater can judge tone, fluency, and
whether an answer resolved the question; they cannot judge what was accurate or
what leaves the user sharper next week. That information isn't available at the
moment of judgment. The gap between "what a fast pick can see" and "what was
actually true" is the myopia — emergent from the reward structure, not a switch.

## Item bank (grounded in real dataset rows)

8 pairs, each **anchored to a real row** from the cited datasets, with the
`source` field naming the dataset + base row:

- SycophancyEval (Sharma et al. 2023/2024, arXiv 2310.13548) —
  `meg-tong/sycophancy-eval`: `feedback.jsonl` (the renewable-energy op-ed,
  rating 1, tagged "strawman fallacy"; the dice-probability solution, correct
  625/648 vs wrong 625/647), `are_you_sure.jsonl` (`aqua_mc` MC questions with
  ground-truth letters), `answer.jsonl` (the Peter Principle trivia, correct
  "The Peter Principle" vs the dataset's fabricated "Jones Theory").
- Perez et al. 2022 (arXiv 2212.09251) — `anthropics/evals`: the political
  typology persona row.

**What's real vs synthesized.** The prompts and the ground truth (correct
answer, logical error, rating) are read straight from the repos {PRIMARY-FULL,
2026-06-29}. Those datasets are *measurement probes*, not pairwise annotator
tasks, and contain no ready-made A/B responses — and no public set is both real
human A/B picks and sycophancy-labelled. So the two response **texts** are
synthesized, each anchored to the row's real correct/incorrect answer {response
wording is Claude's, flagged in `items.js`}.

**Realistic base rate, not leading.** Agreeing with the user is often correct,
so 5 of 8 items are ones where the agreeable answer is *also* the truthful one
(user states a correct belief; shares correct work; is right to doubt a wrong
answer; is right to be skeptical of a flawed argument). Only 3 diverge — the
op-ed praise, the caved geometry answer, the agreed-with falsehood. This avoids
the earlier "agreeable = always wrong" feel: the agreement weight is learned
from the common case and misfires on the tail. `truthful` is deliberately **not
predictable** from the visible features (agreeable answers are sometimes right,
sometimes wrong), which is what makes it the hidden column. The agreeable side
alternates A/B so the pull is never positional. Verified by the Node harness in
the build notes: agreement is the top recovered weight; 3 of 8 contested.

## State & persistence

- `sessionStorage` key `annotator:state` holds `{round, reached, order, picks}`.
  Picks survive a reload within the tab session but reset on a fresh visit —
  repeatable without polluting `localStorage` (theme stays in `localStorage`).
- Single linear flow; **no deep-linking / per-phase URLs** (decided with Irwin).
- Presentation order is shuffled on a fresh visit and on "Annotate again".

## Conventions followed

- Design tokens reused verbatim from `tokens.astro`/`corpus.astro`; added only
  `--an-true` (blue secondary) for the held-out-helpfulness meter.
- Fonts: Outfit (UI), Alegreya (serif body — matching the sibling explainers,
  which load Alegreya rather than Literata), Sono (mono). This matches the
  model page deliberately for visual consistency across the standalone figures.
- Swiss/minimal/flat: no rounded corners, gradients, or drop shadows.
- Repo writing rule: Claude's suggested editorial copy is rendered inside
  `<code class="ph">` so Irwin can distinguish it from his own writing. The
  closing line is Irwin's, quoted from `[[The Reinforcement Substrate]]`. The
  item-bank dialogue is figure data, not authored prose. No "it's not X, it's Y".
- Source Transparency: the five core papers are cited inline where the UI leans
  on them, with a Sources `<details>` listing arXiv IDs and what each backs.
